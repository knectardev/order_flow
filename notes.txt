

While the plan is robust, here are three specific areas where the implementation might face friction during your Cursor/Claude workflow:

#### 1. The "Floating Point" Gate (Phase 1e)
You specified `math.isclose(abs_tol=1e-9)` as a hard gate. 
* **The Risk:** Python’s `pandas` (DuckDB source) and JavaScript’s `JSON.parse` handle floating-point precision slightly differently during serialization. 
* **The Fix:** If the verify script fails on 15th-decimal-place noise, don't loosen the tolerance to $1e-5$. Instead, ensure the aggregator **rounds** `vpt` and `concentration` to a fixed precision (e.g., 6 decimal places) *before* writing to both DuckDB and JSON. This ensures the "Hard Gate" remains meaningful without being tripped by floating-point "jitter."

#### 2. The "30-Bar Warmup" UX (Phase 2d)
The plan calls for an amber `WARMING UP` label and 0.4 opacity. 
* **Refinement:** Ensure the **Event Log sticky row** (`SYSTEM · regime warming up`) is truly pinned. If the user scrolls the log, that system message should stay at the top. This is the "User Trust" layer—without it, a user might think the algorithm has crashed during the first 30 minutes of the RTH session because the matrix isn't "moving."

#### 3. DuckDB Tuple-IN Query (Phase 4a)
The plan uses `(v_rank, d_rank) IN ?` for the brushing filter.
* **Optimization:** This is significantly faster than a series of `OR` statements, but it relies on a multi-column index. Ensure your Phase 1a `init_schema` explicitly creates:
    `CREATE INDEX idx_bars_rank ON bars (v_rank, d_rank);`
* Without this index, "All Sessions" occupancy queries or multi-cell brushing will degrade into full table scans as your DuckDB file grows over months of data.
