
### Three "Integrity Checks" to add to your workflow
While the plan is sound, keep these three risks in mind as you build:

#### 1. The "Boundary Condition" Test
What happens when `range_pct` or `v_rank` hits its extreme (e.g., 0.0 or the theoretical max)? 
* **The Check:** Add a test case for `regime_exit_scale.py` that passes "garbage" regime values (NaN, negative, or extremely high values). Your helper should return the *template* (fallback) value rather than blowing up the `BacktestEngine` or producing a 0-tick stop loss.

#### 2. The Null Hypothesis Consistency
Since your Null Hypothesis (NH) parity loop schedules trades based on eligible indices, ensure that the **regime-scaling is applied during the NH run exactly as it is in the baseline run.** * Because your scaling is deterministic (based on bar data at entry time), the NH parity loop *should* handle this naturally. However, verify that your NH parity test doesn't accidentally pick up "average" regime data. It must use the *exact* regime data of the randomized fire time.

#### 3. Execution/Slippage Interaction
When you scale your SL/TP based on volatility, you are essentially tightening or widening your "Exit Zone." 
* **The Risk:** If you tighten your stop loss significantly during low-volatility regimes, you might find that `slippage_ticks` becomes a larger percentage of your risk.
* **The Advice:** Keep an eye on your "Slippage-to-Stop-Loss" ratio in your metadata. If your SL gets dynamically scaled to be very tight (e.g., 4 ticks) and your `slippage_ticks` is 2, your exit execution is essentially 50% slippage-dominated. This might make the strategy look "unprofitable" during low volatility simply because of slippage mechanics, not because of the signal logic.
